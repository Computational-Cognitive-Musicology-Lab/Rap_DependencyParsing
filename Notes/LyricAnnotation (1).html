CoCoPops Lyrics Annotations

CoCoPops annotations include detailed information about the music’s lyrics, in the following spines:

  • **stress for prosodic stress
  • **ipa pronunciation
  • **silbe for lexical information and basic syntax boundaries.
  • **depend for syntactic dependences.
  • **POS for part of speech.
  • **Rhyme

Lexical Information

The **silbe spine encodes lexical information (as well as high-level basic syntactic/semantic grouping. Details below).

Each syllable is represented in its own record. However, syllables are grouped into lexical items. In most cases, lexical items are words. However, the concept of a “word” as applied in standard English orthography and parlance does not necessarily describe a coherent linguistic element. Many multi-word sequences in English are best considered a single unit of semantic/syntactic meaning: examples include “traffic light,” “by the way,” and “motor vehicle.” Such multi-word sequences are single lexical items.

Multi-syllable lexical items are separated by either - (within word) or _ (between words): these characters (- or _) are attached to each syllable in a multi-syllable unit as either a prefix (first syllable of an item), affix (last syllable of an item), or both (middle syllable). How characters are split across words is generally irrelevant—your general guidelines are too first maximize consistency, and two maximize readability. Reflecting pronunciation is not, and should not, be a goal (more below). The word “because” should be split be- -cause always, regardless of pronunciation (unless it is shortened to one syllable! Syntax

Syntactic information is encoded in two places: high-level grouping information is encoded as punctuation in the **silbe spine, while the full detail of syntactic dependency is encoded in the **depend spine.

Punctuation

**silbe indicates high level syntactic groupings using punctuation. Note that the use of punctuation does not exactly match standard usage in formal or informal writing.

Within a lyric, the end of syntactic units are marked at the end of the last syllable in the syntactic unit. These punctuation divide the lyrics into syntactically independent units.

Complete syntactic closure is marked with .—this indicates that there is no syntactic dependency across this boundary. Syntactic units that are connected by explicit coordinating conjunctions (e.g., “and”) are closed with a ,, with the , placed on the last syllable before the coordinating conjunction.

In the normative, case syntactic units are “complete” clauses, consisting of a subject (noun phrase) and predicate (verb phrase). However, it is non uncommon in lyrics to have list of noun, verb, or even prepositional phrases, which are not explicitely connected into a larger structure. In many cases, we can interpret such lists as examples of asyndetic coordination—coordination with an elided (non explicit) conjunction. We use ; to indicate boundaries between independent phrases: we can interpret this ; as “either a period or a comma.” These cases should be associated with parataxis or list tag in **depend.

**depend

Though basic syntactic units are indicated in **silbe punctuation, detailed syntactic information is indicated in the **depend spine. **depend annotations are aligned with the first syllable of each lexical item—later syllables/words are simply null tokens. Each **depend token indicates a dependency type and a head word. Each token begins with a dependency type: the decency types are listed below, followed by an arrow (> or <) indicating the relative position (forward or backward in time) of the head word relative to the current (dependent) word. After the arrow, the entire head word is written out; For example, >foo indicates that the next instance of the word “foo” is the head, while <foo indicates that the previous instance of the word “foo” is the head.

Elision

In artistic language, it is common for certain words to be elided—not spoken, but still by implicitly understood. Asyndetic coordination (examples above) is a clear case, with elided conjunctions.

In general cases of elided constructs, we use (). An empty () can be used to indicate “something elided” or the parentheses can be filled with a postulated elided words. Notice that by writing (), an annotator is indicating that there is not a syntactic boundary here. These cases should be associated with orphan tags in **depend.

Ambiguity

In some cases, syntax is truly ambiguous. The () is used to indicate ambiguity. Other punctuation can be placed inside (), such as (.) or (,). This indicates there “may or may not be” a boundary here. If there is ambiguity between two possiblities, place a | between possiblities: (.|,).

In many cases, ambiguity is not constrained two one syllable, but two (or more) interpretations of a larger span. In this case, the (x|y) pattern is broken across multiple lines.

In **depend, ambiguous syntactic parses should be split into two (or more) spine paths. To make the difference between paths clear, paths should encompass the entire area of possible syntactic dependency, even if there is redundancy between parts of the paths. In other words, even if only a few words are ambiguous, two entirely seperate syntactic parses should be written to cover all the surrounding dependencies—paths should never refer to dependencies outside the path. Tags

Many utterances are proceeded, succeeded, or intersperesed by elements which are not clearly linked to the syntactic structure of the sentence, but serve an expressive role. These are marked with the discourse tag. One example is saying “Well” or “Gosh” before beginning a sentence. Another example is “tag questions,” as in “You aren’t going to the beach are you?.” These are associated with vocative and discourse tags in **depend.

Non syntactic elements that are not clearly “attached” to a syntactic unit, should be marked with !, on each and every element! This could include expletives. Tags that are associated with a unit are marked with either / (prefix) or \ (affix)—both / and \ are used for infixes:

Annotation Workflow

Our current approach to constructing CoCoPops-style lyrics annotations is as follows. Our work flow combines human annotation with automated steps.

**silbe

  1. Lyrics are input directly into melodic transcriptions, indicating musical alignment (number of syllables, melisma, etc.).
  2. Lyrics are automatically converted to all lower-case and punctuation is removed.
  3. Lyrics are spot checked and edited for:
  • Correctness (referencing music itself and/or websites)
  • Proper nouns are capitalized.
  • Multi-word lexical items are indicated (adding _ as necessary).
  • Convert to standard spelling.
  1. Syntactic punctuation is added.

**depend and **pos

Once **silbe spines are prepared, we further prepare the text for automatic analysis. Since lyrics make use of many slang words and proper nouns, we preprocess the **silbe lyrics:

  1. Copy **silbe into a new spine.
  2. In the new spine begin manually converting slang and unusual words to similar words of the same POS which match the dependency structure. For example, “cheddar” could be translated to “money”—depending on context of course!
    • Proper names can be converted to common names like “Mary Smith.”
    • Multi-word lexical items should be collapsed to a single word representing a similar meaning/category.
  3. Parentheticals are removed from elisions, wherever possible, forming coherent syntactic utterances.
  4. For any ambiguous passages, the annotator chooses one analysis and removes alternative punctuation.

Once the preprocessed spine is complete, we use automated functions (humdrum toolkit) to collapse syllables to single words, and collapse and syntactic units (marked by either ., ;, :, or , into single lines). Care must be taken to make sure that the original position of the first syllable of each lexical item in the output.

  1. The text lines from the previous step are fed into the Stanford dependency parser, to generate dependency and POS tags.
  2. The Stanford parsers’ output is converted to our target format (i.e., det>ladies).
  3. The dependency and POS outputs are padded with null tokens and realign with the humdrum data.

In the final step, the **depend and **pos data is spot-checked by human annotators.
